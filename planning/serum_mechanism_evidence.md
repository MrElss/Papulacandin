# Serum-inactivation mechanism — external literature evidence

*Assembled from connected literature tools (Consensus, PubMed) to answer the open
question from the Stage-1 discussion: **why** do papulacandins lose activity in
serum, and can `potency × free-fraction × stability` fully capture it?*

## Key finding: the serum shift is NOT explained by protein binding

- The echinocandin serum-MIC increase does **not** track % protein binding.
  Caspofungin (~97% bound) is modestly affected; micafungin / anidulafungin
  (~99% bound) shift 32–256×. Authors explicitly attribute the effect to "other
  factors and/or secondary mechanisms," not binding rate
  (Elefanti 2013, AAC; Odabaşı 2007, AAC).
- Part of the effect is a **direct action of serum on the target**: "serum
  decreased the sensitivity of glucan synthase to echinocandin drugs"
  (Paderu 2007, AAC — "Serum Differentially Alters the Antifungal Properties of
  Echinocandin Drugs").
- **Papulacandin-specific:** serum **albumin partially protects glucan synthase
  from papulacandin B**, which acts partly by "non-specific detergent-like
  disruption of the membrane environment" (Ko, Frost et al. 1994, BBA;
  doi:10.1016/0005-2736(94)90329-8). => the loss is **amphiphile sequestration by
  albumin / fatty-acid-binding proteins**, tied to lipophilic/detergent character
  — not classical reversible drug–albumin binding, and not (primarily) degradation.
- In-vitro serum activity **correlates with animal-model outcomes** for
  echinocandins (Nasar 2013, Curr Fungal Infect Rep) — validating serum-MIC ↔
  in-vivo efficacy, i.e. the basis for the in-vivo-as-serum-active proxy.

## Consequences for the project

1. **`potency × fu × stability` is incomplete.** A direct serum→target term exists
   that no compound descriptor predicts. Solving fu and stability does NOT
   guarantee serum activity. Predict less; measure the serum outcome directly.
2. **Leading papulacandin mechanism = amphiphile sequestration** (albumin /
   lipoprotein partitioning driven by lipophilicity), plus a membrane/target
   effect. The esterase-degradation hypothesis is demoted to secondary (no direct
   evidence; the C-6′ ester SAR is equally a partitioning lever).
3. **Design axis = modulate lipophilicity / partitioning**, not primarily ester
   stability. Fusacandin C-6′ ester SAR reads as a partitioning effect.
4. **Assay = serum- or albumin-shift MIC** (±50% serum, ±albumin), a direct binary
   retained/lost readout — cheaper and more mechanism-relevant than
   equilibrium-dialysis fu, which may miss lipoprotein/detergent sequestration.
5. **Binary, measurement-first endpoint (the Q4 reframe) is the right call**, now
   backed by the serum-MIC ↔ in-vivo link.
